Types of Networks.

Local Networks

Local networks come in all shapes and sizes. They can range from simple networks consisting of two computers to networks connecting hundreds of thousands of devices.
Networks installed in small offices or homes are often referred to as small office/home office (SOHO) networks.
SOHO networks let you share resources and information, including files and videos, between a few local users in the general vicinity

Large Networks

Large networks can be used to advertise and sell products, order supplies, and communicate with customers or other districts.
Communication over a network is usually more efficient and less expensive than traditional means of communication, such as snail mail or long-distance phone calls.
Networks enable instant communication through email and instant messaging, and provide access to data and information stored on network servers or other devices.
Large (enterprise) and SOHO networks usually provide a shared connection to the internet (connection of thousands of networks).
The internet is considered a "network of networks" because it is composed of hundreds of local networks that are interconnected, forming larger and larger networks (also known as the internet).

Data Transmission

Types of data

Voluntered data

This type of data is the type you voluntarily give away, eg, uploading things to Google Drive, sending a text, etc.

Inferred data

This type of data you give away without realizing for instance, using your credit card, when a transaction happens, your credit card data is transferred to the store and your bank.
Your bank uses that data to gather information about you, your decisions, where you shop, etc.

Observed Data

This type of data is typically automatically collected by the device you use, for example, your location (if you have your location services turned on).
This data can be used by your cell phone provider to pinpoint where you are.

Binary

Computers and networks only work with binary digits, also called bits, zeros, and ones. It can be difficult to imagine that all of our data is stored and transmitted as a series of bits.
Each bit can only have one of two possible values, 0 or 1.
Bits represent the smallest piece of data a computer can use.
Humans interpret words and pictures, but computers can only interpret in patterns of bits or 1s and 0s.
A bit is stored and transmitted as one of two possible discrete states.
For example, a light switch can be either On or Off; in binary representation, these states would correspond to 1 and 0. Every input device (mouse, keyboard, mic, etc) will translate human interaction into binary code for the CPU to process and send to a storage device to store.
Every output device (printer, speakers, monitors, etc.) will take binary data and translate it back into human-readable form. Within the computer itself, all data is processed and stored as binary.
Computers use binary codes to symbolize and interpret letters, numbers, and special characters with bits. A commonly used code is the American Standard Code for Information Interchange (ASCII).
Which can store letters, numbers, and special characters with bits.
For example:
The letter: Z = 1111010
The Number: 6 = 01100110
The Special character: # = 00100011
Each group of eight bits is known as a byte. Bits and bytes can be used to represent almost any type of information digitally, including analog data, graphics, photos, audio, video, and music.
